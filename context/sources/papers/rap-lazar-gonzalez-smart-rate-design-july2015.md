## Executive Summary

### Introduction

For most of its history, the electric utility industry saw little change in the economic and physical operating characteristics of the electric system. Though the system provided reliable and low-cost service, little in terms of system status or customer use was known in real or near real time. For an industry in the information age, parts of the electric system can be considered rather "unenlightened."

Current advancements in technology will have marked impact on current and future rate designs. First, end-users (i.e., customers) are installing their own generation, mostly in the form of photovoltaic (PV) systems, and are connecting different types of end-use appliances with increasing "intelligence" built in; electric vehicles (EVs), too, are poised to grow rapidly as a whole new class of end-use, just as storage systems are poised to become economic. Second, utilities are deploying advanced metering and associated data systems, sometimes referred to as advanced metering infrastructure (AMI) or smart meters, and more sophisticated supervisory control and data acquisition (SCADA) systems to monitor system operations. To realize the full potential of these new systems and end-uses, regulators, utilities, third-party service providers, and customers will need to utilize more advanced rate designs than they have in the past.

Rate design is the regulatory term used to describe the pricing structure reflected in customer bills and used by electric utilities in the United States. Rate design is not only the itemized prices set forth in tariffs; it is also the underlying theory and process used to derive those prices. Rate design is important because the structure of prices — that is, the form and periodicity of prices for the various services offered by a regulated company — has a profound impact on the choices made by customers, utilities, and other electric market participants. The structure of rate designs and the prices set by these designs can either encourage or discourage usage at certain times of the day, for example, which in turn affects resource development and utilization choices. It can also affect the amount of electricity customers consume and their attention to conservation. These choices then have indirect consequences in terms of total costs and benefits to society, environmental and health impacts, and the overall economy.[^1]

[^1]: Weston, F. (2000). _Charging for Distribution Utility Services: Issues in Rate Design_. Montpelier, VT: The Regulatory Assistance Project. Available at: [http://www.raponline.org/document/download/id/412](http://www.raponline.org/document/download/id/412)

Despite its critical importance, rate design is poorly understood by the general public and often lacks transparency. The difference between a progressive and regressive design can have a large effect — 15 percent by one estimate, but it could be more — on customer usage.[^2] Traditional rate designs, which charge a single rate per unit of consumption (or worse, lower that rate as consumption increases) may not serve consumers or society best. As advancements in technology and customer preferences evolve, the industry must adapt to change or risk the fate of landline telephone companies, which have lost 60 percent of their access lines since the advent of telecommunications competition.[^3]

[^2]: Lazar, J. (2013). _Rate Design Where Advanced Metering Infrastructure Has Not Been Fully Deployed_. Montpelier, VT: The Regulatory Assistance Project. Available at: [http://www.raponline.org/document/download/id/6516](http://www.raponline.org/document/download/id/6516). Appendix A provides a calculation of how rate design can influence consumption.

[^3]: Federal Communications Commission (2014, October). _Local Telephone Competition Report_, available at: [https://www.fcc.gov/encyclopedia/local-telephone-competition-reports](https://www.fcc.gov/encyclopedia/local-telephone-competition-reports)

Rate design relies in strong measure upon the judicious application of certain economic guidelines. The following elements of economically efficient rate design that are necessary to address current and coming challenges in the electric industry are based on those laid out in James Bonbright's 1961 _Principles of Public Utility Rates_, and in Garfield and Lovejoy's 1964 _Public Utility Economics_. These principles require that rates should:

- Be forward-looking and reflect long-run marginal costs;
- Focus on the usage components of service, which are the most cost- and price-sensitive;
- Be simple and understandable;
- Recover system costs in proportion to how much electricity consumers use, and when they use it;
- Give consumers appropriate information and the opportunity to respond by adjusting usage; and
- Where possible, be temporally and geographically dynamic.[^4]

[^4]: Lazar, 2013, p. 10.

Rates can be designed to meet (or, in the case of poor rate design, frustrate) public policy objectives to use electricity more efficiently, meet environmental goals, and minimize adverse social impacts, including public health, among others. They are also pivotal in providing utilities the opportunity to recover their authorized revenue requirement. Revenue adequacy is a core objective of rate design, but the more constructive design ideal for rates is forward-looking, so that future investment decisions by the utility and by customers can be harmonized.

Based on these historical works, and looking forward to a world with high levels of energy efficiency, distributed generation (DG), and customer options for onsite backup supply, the following **three fundamental principles** should be considered for modern rate design:

> **Principle 1**: A customer should be able to connect to the grid for no more than the cost of connecting to the grid.
>
> **Principle 2**: Customers should pay for grid services and power supply in proportion to how much they use these services and how much power they consume.
>
> **Principle 3**: Customers who supply power to the grid should be fairly compensated for the full value of the power they supply.

These principles and priorities should be reflected in smarter rates designed to maximize the value of technology innovations, open up new markets, and accommodate the distribution and diversification of customer-sited generation resources. This necessarily includes consideration of what those future technologies and policies could look like, with a focus on metering and billing, market structure, and pricing. In particular, rate design should provide a "price signal" to customers, utilities, and other market participants to inform their consumption and investment decisions regarding energy efficiency, demand response (DR), and DG, collectively referred to as distributed energy resources (DER). **Bidirectional, time-sensitive prices that more accurately reflect costs most closely align with the principles of modern rate design.**

### Challenges in Utility Rate Design

Over the last two decades, federal, state, and local policymakers have implemented policies that have spurred the development of customer-sited DG, in particular customer-sited PV systems. The policies range from federal tax credits to state renewable portfolio standards, net energy metering (NEM), and interconnection standards.[^5]

[^5]: Steward, D., & Doris, E. (2014, November). _The Effect of State Policy Suites on the Development of Solar Markets_. NREL. See also the Energy Department's SunShot Initiative at: [http://www.energy.gov/sunshot](http://www.energy.gov/sunshot).

As the costs of renewable and other DG technologies — wind turbines, small hydro, biomass, and others — have decreased, the options available to customers to procure these technologies have increased. DG systems are decentralized, modular, and more flexible technologies that are located close to the load they serve. Customers can typically purchase or lease the DG from a third party, often with seller or third-party financing.

At low levels of installation of distributed renewables (e.g., under five percent of customers), few if any physical modifications are required to electric distribution systems.[^6] The scenario changes once solar output exceeds total load on a given substation. Hawaii, with the highest PV penetration of any state and where more than ten percent of residential consumers have PV systems installed, is serving as a laboratory as it adapts to a high-renewable environment.

[^6]: Hawaiian Electric Company, with 11-percent PV saturation, is just now beginning to invest in distribution system modifications.

In addition, EVs are a small part of the electricity load currently, but growth in the sector is likely. Because of the presence of batteries in the vehicles and the ability to control the timing of when they are charged, EV loads can be very different from traditional loads. This requires time-sensitive pricing.

Microgrids will be another near-future challenge. Storage technologies such as Tesla's Powerwall battery could be a game changer. The presence of generation, storage, and smart control technologies at customer premises offers the opportunity for customers to provide "ancillary services" including voltage regulation, power factor control, frequency control, and spinning reserves. Rate design can either enable these values to be garnered or erect barriers to them.

### Rate Design in Theory and Practice

#### Balancing Stakeholder Interests

A variety of stakeholder interests are at play in the debate over rate design. Regulators face the task of fairly balancing concerns among utilities, consumers and their advocates, industry interests, unregulated power plant owners, and societal interests.

#### Reaffirming the Principles of Rate Design in the Wake of Change

Good rate design should work in concert with the industry's clean technologic innovations and institutional changes. Best practice rate design solutions should balance the goals of:

- Assuring recovery of utility prudently incurred costs;
- Maintaining grid reliability;
- Assuring fairness to all customer classes and sub-classes;
- Assisting the transition of the industry to a clean-energy future;
- Setting economically efficient prices that are forward-looking and lead to the optimum allocation of utility and customer resources;
- Maximizing the value and effectiveness of new technologies as they become available and are deployed on, or alongside, the electric system; and
- Preventing anticompetitive or anti-innovation market structures or behavior.

#### Evaluating and Allocating Costs

The design of rates begins with a functional evaluation of the costs incurred by the utility to provide service to its customers — customer costs, distribution costs, and power supply and transmission costs. A critical step is the allocation of costs among different customer classes. Once the customer class revenue burdens are determined, prices must be set to generate those revenues. The general principle that the cost-causer should pay prices that cover the costs he or she causes might also suggest that the nature of the causation and the form of the price are critically related: costs that vary directly with energy usage are recovered in energy (kWh) charges, costs driven by peak demands are recovered in demand charges or time-varying kWh charges, and customer-specific costs unrelated to usage are recovered in customer charges.

### Basic Rate Designs

**Customer charges** are per-month fixed charges that apply to each customer in a tariff class, regardless of their usage. **High customer charges impose unfair costs on small-use residential consumers, including most low-income households and apartment residents.** The fixed charge for residential or commercial service should not exceed the customer-specific costs attributable to an incremental consumer.

**Demand charges** are commonly used to recover some costs of generation, transmission, and distribution of large commercial and industrial customers. Because traditional demand charges are measured on the basis of the individual customer's peak, regardless of whether it coincides with the peaks on any portion of the system, this approach inevitably results in a mismatch between the costs incurred to serve the customer and the prices charged. Movement away from demand charges, toward more granular time-varying energy rates, is appropriate.

**A few rate analysts have recommended that demand charges be extended to residential consumers.** This option is inapt for most situations: the only distribution system component sized to individual customer demands is the final line transformer (about $1/kW/month). Time-differentiated prices can more equitably recover costs that are actually peak-oriented from all customers, including solar customers.

**Energy charges** are per-kWh charges for electricity consumed. Time-varying (and, eventually, more dynamic) energy charges are the best way to reflect costs to consumers and to encourage efficient use of electricity.

### Time-Varying Rates

It is hard to envision an electric system future without greater use of time-differentiated pricing. **TOU rates** define a multi-hour time of the day as "on-peak," during which prices are higher. They are an improvement over flat or inclining block rates because they offer some correlation between temporally changing costs and actual consumption. However, concentrating peak-related charges into as few hours as possible produces a better customer response.

**Critical peak pricing (CPP)** sets prices during a limited number of specific "critical peak periods" at much higher prices. The customer is given advance notice of critical peak days. CPP has been largely successful and evidence supports setting these as default rates for large groups of consumers.

**Peak-time rebate (PTR)** gives customers a large credit on their bills if they can reduce usage during a peak-time event. PTR is distinguishable from CPP in that it is voluntary. Both require the use of an interval meter or smart meter.

**Real-time pricing (RTP)** charges the customer the actual prices being set in wholesale markets as they vary hour by hour.

### Rates to Compensate DG

**Feed-in tariffs (FIT)** pay a premium price for renewable energy based on the cost of the resources. **A value of solar tariff (VOST)** compensates the solar provider on the basis of the value provided. **Net energy metering (NEM)** measures the customer's net usage from the grid and charges at the standard tariff price. For utilities where only a small percentage of consumers have installed solar, a simple NEM option will generally be easier to measure, more acceptable to consumers, and simpler to administer.

The three principles suggest:

- Only customer-specific costs should be applied to the bill for connecting to the grid.
- The cost for use of the distribution grid should be charged in relation to customer purchases of energy.
- Time-varying rates are appropriate in both directions of the transaction.
- DG customers should be free from discrimination.

### Rate Designs That Discourage DG

**A minimum bill** charges a minimum fixed charge entitling the customer to a minimum amount of energy. Not typically considered good rate design.

**Straight fixed/variable (SFV) design**: Utilities sharply increase monthly fixed charges with offsetting reductions to the per-unit price. This deviates from long-established rate design principles and sharply increases bills for apartment dwellers, urban consumers, highly efficient homes, and customers with DG systems, while benefiting larger homes and suburban and rural customers.

**Distribution system cost surcharges**: Some states are imposing new fees on DG customers. **Exit fees** are charges imposed on consumers who cease taking utility service; as a general rule, they are inappropriate rate design measures.

### Enabling Smart Technology

Utilities from Maine to California have deployed smart grid upgrades. Smart meters provide data acquisition, equipment control, and communication capability between the customer and the power grid. Smart meter deployment is expected to reach 91 percent of the United States by 2022. However, merely installing smart meters does not alone facilitate advanced pricing; MDMS investments, billing engine modifications, and sophisticated rate studies are also needed.

Various technology enhancements can improve the effectiveness of more complex rate designs by enabling customers to respond to prices automatically: smart thermostats, grid-integrated water heating, EV chargers, and vehicle-to-grid applications.

The costs associated with smart grid investments should be apportioned so that the costs are shared by all aspects of utility service that benefit. Smart meter functions should not be recovered exclusively as customer-related costs.

### Implementing Smart Rates

"Smart rates" include TOU, PTR, CPP, and RTP (all with and without technology). Currently most utilities that have smart rates offer them as optional services. Evidence supports default (opt-out) implementation with customer protections.

Evidence shows that advanced pricing works best with technology enhancement to enable automated response to higher prices. Over 200 time-differentiated rate tests have been conducted worldwide. The consensus is that customers respond to prices, and enabling technologies enhance price responsiveness. TOU and CPP rates may also be more fair to customers than traditional flat rates.

### Electric Vehicles

Even without AMI deployment, interval TOU meters can allow EVs to be separately metered. A utility with AMI has many options for providing an EV rate: a simple TOU rate, a multi-period TOU rate with a super-off-peak period, a critical peak pricing rate, or a real-time price. For public charging stations, a wide variety of pricing schemes are used, from free charging to hourly parking to TOU rates.

### Policies to Complement Smart Rate Design

Utilities find themselves at a crossroads. The smart future requires:

- Utility regulators adopting time-varying and dynamic rate designs, with consumer education, shadow billing, a "hold harmless" provision, and excellent customer support.
- Some form of revenue regulation to ensure fair utility returns.
- State building energy codes requiring home energy management systems.
- Customer-sited generation including smart inverters and customer-sited batteries.
- Federal appliance standards requiring control technologies in new major appliances.

**The "not-so-smart" future** would involve movement toward high recurring fixed charges. They provide utilities with stable revenues but punish lower-usage customers, discourage efficiency improvements and adoption of distributed renewables, and over time can lead to unnecessary increases in consumption or promote customer grid defection. They are economically inefficient and inequitable and are not justified by any fundamental principle of neoclassical economic theory. They are, in fact, nothing more than a government-sanctioned exercise of monopoly power.

Utility cost recovery and revenue stability can be addressed through a higher allowed rate of return, incentive regulation (performance-based ratemaking), or **revenue decoupling** — the best option for addressing utility revenue attrition.

### Conclusion (Executive Summary)

Rate design will be an important driver of utilities' success in making the transition to a clean power system. Smart rate designs will need to address not only the amount consumed but also when it is consumed and its impact on costs and other customers. Viewed as a quick fix to lost revenues, SFV rates with high monthly fixed charges are increasingly being proposed by utilities. SFV is not a step forward, but a step backward. It discourages innovation and efficiency, penalizes low-income and apartment residents, and results in per-unit prices that fall far short of total system long-run incremental costs.

## I. Introduction

For most of its history, the electric utility industry saw little change in the economic and physical operating characteristics of the electric system. Large central station generating plants connected to high-voltage transmission delivered power to local distribution grids for delivery to end users, mostly by vertically integrated utilities. Though reliable and remarkably low-cost, the historical electric system was, and in many ways remains, a black box to both customers and to system operators.[^i1]

[^i1]: Those interested in the emerging changes and the challenges they present are invited to go directly to the sections covering Rate Design Principles and Rate Design for Specific Applications.

Today, the industry is facing radical changes. Information systems are coming to the grid that will inform customers and system operators about how the system really works. Two categories of changes will both demand and allow more sophisticated pricing:

1. End users are installing their own generation (especially PV), connecting smart end-use appliances, and EVs and energy storage are poised to grow rapidly.
2. Utilities are deploying AMI and sophisticated SCADA systems.

To realize the full potential of these new systems, more advanced rate designs are needed. Most important will be the more widespread use of **bidirectional, time-sensitive prices** that more accurately reflect cost.

### Basics of Rate Design

Rate design is the regulatory term used to describe the pricing structure used by electric utilities. It explicitly includes the itemized prices set forth in tariffs and implicitly includes the underlying theory and process used to derive those prices. The structure of prices has an impact on the choices made by customers, utilities, and other market participants, which in turn affect resource development and utilization choices.[^i2]

Rate design is poorly understood by the general public and often lacks transparency.[^i3] The difference between a progressive and regressive rate design can increase customer usage by as much as 15 percent.[^i4] Rate design determines the prices consumers see and use to guide their consumption and investment choices.[^i5]

> **Principle 2**: Customers should pay for grid services and power supply in proportion to how much they use these services, and how much power they consume.
>
> **Principle 3**: Customers that supply power to the grid should be fairly compensated for the full value of the power they supply.[^i7]

## III. Rate Design to Enable "Smart" Technology

### Survey of Technology

Utilities from Maine to California have deployed smart grid upgrades.[^iii29] These promise to deliver an entirely new level of information about system operations and consumer behavior.[^iii30] Computerizing the traditional grid with AMI and advanced SCADA systems will enable the development of new and dynamic rate offerings. Meanwhile, smart home appliances that can automatically respond to prices or be dispatched by system operators or third-party service providers will assist customers in managing their usage and minimize total system costs and increase system reliability.[^iii31] Various technology enhancements can improve the effectiveness of more complex rate designs:

- **Smart thermostats**: Can automatically change heating and cooling settings in response to real-time price changes.
- **Grid-integrated water heating**: Can automatically increase hot water storage during low-cost periods, curtail water heating operation during high-cost periods, and supply ancillary services to the utility without the consumer even noticing.[^iii32]
- **Electric vehicle chargers**: Can be programmed to provide "economy" charges, allowing the customer to take advantage of low-cost energy when it is available.
- **Vehicle-to-grid applications**: Can enable EV batteries to flow power back to the grid during critical hours, essentially allowing the grid operator use of the EV batteries and provide a means of compensation to EV owners for supplying the energy.[^iii33]

#### Smart Meters

Smart meters provide data acquisition, equipment control, and communication capability between the customer and the power grid, plus outage detection and reduced meter reading costs.[^iii34] They record customer usage at a fine timescale and communicate information back to the utility and customer. Smart meters are needed when the rate design requires knowing when power is flowing and in which direction, to more accurately value the cost of customer use and the value of customer production.[^iii35]

> **Remote Disconnection and Reconnection: Challenge and Opportunity**
>
> Without smart meters, when utilities disconnect service (move-out, or non-payment), they must send a service person to the premises to lock out the meter. This has a cost, normally recovered through a levy on the individual consumer. Where disconnections are effected for non-payment, it often (depending on regulatory commission rules) involves three site visits: one to post the notice of impending disconnection, one to effect disconnection, and a third to reconnect. The second and third site visits reduce the likelihood of disconnection by providing an opportunity for the consumer to make a payment at the site to avoid disconnection. With smart meters, the disconnection and reconnection can be done remotely. This has an economic benefit, but raises a social equity concern. The challenge is to realize the operational benefit of the remote disconnect and reconnect while maintaining safeguards for vulnerable populations.
>
> Low-income advocates have a concern about this capability, because disconnection can be done without any site visit, and customers with medical needs, or who have the ability to make a field payment, are disconnected. Some utilities with remote disconnect capability have addressed this by having the site visit performed by a (lower paid and more customer-oriented) customer service agent who is better able to judge an exception or accept field payment, rather than by a (more technically trained) electrical worker. This can provide lower costs and better service than previous approaches, and avoid one or two site visits. In any event, with remote reconnection, it is possible for a customer to phone in a payment, and have service restored immediately. Regulators are becoming aware of both the promise and pitfalls of this remote capability. In some foreign countries, money transfer via prepaid cellular phone systems enables immediate payment even for consumers without credit cards or bank accounts. Further, the charge for disconnection and reconnection to the consumer should be dramatically reduced to reflect the reduced costs to the utility.

## IV. Rate Design Principles and Solutions

### Traditional Principles

The design of rates begins with a functional evaluation of the costs incurred by the utility. A foundational notion is to charge customers in relation to the costs incurred to serve them. A critical step is the allocation of costs among different customer classes. In reaching a cost allocation determination, regulators usually will consider different approaches (embedded cost vs. marginal cost, single peak hour or multiple peak hours,[^iv39] etc.) and review different cost of service studies. Rate making is partly science and partly art. Appendix A of this paper addresses these ideas in more detail.[^iv40]

#### Customer Costs

Customer costs are those that vary with the number of customers: metering, billing and collection, and customer assistance — typically no more than $5 to $10 a month per residential consumer. AMI enables functions unassociated with metering or billing; the incremental cost of smart meters above conventional meters should not be recovered exclusively as customer-related costs.

#### Distribution Costs

The basic distribution infrastructure — poles, wires, and transformers — comprises approximately one-quarter of the revenue requirement for the typical electric utility. Although many utilities view these as "fixed costs," in the long run all costs are variable. It is important to recover distribution costs on the basis of end-use consumption.

#### Flat Rates

The simplest form of rate design. A very important principle is to align the incremental price for incremental consumption with long-run incremental costs, including societal costs.[^iv41] This is one reason many utility regulators have implemented inclining block rates — to reflect both utility costs and societal costs in the incremental price per kilowatt-hour.

#### Demand Charges

**Table 2: Typical Commercial Rate with a Demand Charge**

| Rate Element    | Illustrative Rate | How Applied                               |
| --------------- | ----------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Customer Charge | $10/mo            | Independent of usage                      |
| Demand Charge   | $10/kW            | Customer's highest 1-hour usage per month |
| Energy Charge   | $0.10/kWh         | All kWh                                   |

Demand or capacity-related costs are primarily associated with the system peak demand, not the individual customer peak demand.[^iv42] Traditional demand charges based on non-coincident peaks result in a mismatch between costs and prices. A demand "ratchet" (monthly demand charge based on the highest usage during the year) discourages energy efficiency throughout the year.

#### Power Supply Costs

Power supply costs include capital costs of power plants and transmission, fuel and purchased power, and generation and transmission O&M. In restructured markets, power supply costs are nearly all recovered on a time-varying energy basis.

### Principles for Rate Design in the Wake of Change

Good rate design should work in concert with the industry's clean technological innovations and institutional changes. Best practice rate design solutions should balance the same goals listed in the Executive Summary.

#### Stakeholder Interests

**Utility interests**: Utilities tend to see costs as "fixed" and seek reliable recovery methods. High fixed charges are one avenue being pursued.[^iv43] Both the telephone and cable television markets have imposed higher fixed charges. Both have seen significant customer and revenue attrition as customers have moved to competitive and volumetric alternatives.

**Consumer interests**: State consumer advocates generally favor flat rates and low fixed charges.[^iv45] Low-income advocates favor designs with low fixed charges and inclining blocks.[^iv46] California does this for electric and gas rates by defining housing types and climate zones and setting differential baselines.[^iv47] Large industrial users often prefer designs with higher fixed charges and low volumetric rates.

**Solar interests**: The solar industry now employs more people than the coal or nuclear industry in the United States and is not a trivial interest.[^iv48] The solar industry favors net metering, low customer charges, and inclining block rate designs. Current research tends to support the conclusion that the value of solar equals or exceeds the "payment" to customers realized through NEM.[^iv49]

**Unregulated power plant owner interests**: IPPs with coal or nuclear resources are threatened by competing generation. The presence of renewable resources depresses power prices in the middle of the day.[^iv50] IPPs also have a negative view of energy efficiency and DR, as these resources tend to reduce prices in both the wholesale energy and capacity markets.[^iv51]

**Societal interests**: Encompass all market participants plus non-market interests including environmental impacts, fuel cost and supply risk, economic development, and health impacts.

#### Principles Specific to Customer-Sited Solar Rate Design

**Principle 1** (for solar): Only customer-specific costs should be applied to the bill for connecting to the grid. The only truly customer-specific costs on a typical urban/suburban grid are service drops, meters, and billing services.

**Principle 2** (for solar): Nearly all utility services should be priced volumetrically but may vary by time of day, season, and voltage level. Time-varying rates are appropriate in both directions. The only distribution component sized to individual customer demands is the final line transformer. When solar penetration suppresses on-peak prices, some benefits from those price reductions should continue to flow to solar producers.

**Principle 3** (for solar): Prices paid for customer generation must consider avoided production, transmission, distribution, environmental benefits, losses, reserves, fuel cost and supply risk, and other avoided costs. DG customers should be free from discrimination. NEM is a reasonable proxy for the value of solar in the absence of better information.

**Time-of-Use Rates**: Define multi-hour on-peak periods with higher prices. An improvement over flat or inclining block rates. Concentrating peak-related charges into as few hours as possible produces a better customer response.

**Minimum bills**: Charge a minimum fixed charge entitling the customer to a minimum amount of energy. Not considered good rate design, but better than SFV.

**SFV rates**: Sharply increase monthly fixed charges with offsetting reductions to per-unit price. This deviates from long-established rate design principles and accepted economic theory of pricing on the basis of long-run marginal costs. The effect is to sharply increase bills for all low-use customers while benefiting larger homes.

**Distribution system cost surcharges**: Some states are imposing new fees on DG customers.[^iv65] On the other hand, commissions in Idaho, Louisiana, and Utah have rejected fixed charges on solar customers,[^iv66] and California has statutorily limited fixed charges to no more than $10 per month.

**Exit fees**: Generally inappropriate rate design measures. The risk of customer loss is an ordinary business risk for which the utility rate of return is the compensation.

#### General Rate Design Structure

**Demand charges** were implemented for commercial and industrial customers in an era where sophisticated TOU metering was prohibitively expensive. Today, with smart meters and AMI, these costs are trivial. It is more appropriate to utilize more temporally granular time-differentiated rates in lieu of demand charges.[^iv68]

A few rate analysts have recommended that demand charges be extended to small commercial and residential consumers.[^iv69] This option is inapt for most situations for several reasons:

- The only distribution component sized to individual customer demand is the line transformer (a small portion of total cost of service).
- Residential customers have high diversity — different customers use power at different times.
- Customer understanding of demand charges is poor.
- Solar customers may actually contribute power during peak periods.
- Time-varying prices can more equitably recover actual peak-oriented costs.

A monthly fixed charge based on a transformer rental charge may be appropriate (about $1/kW-month), particularly on rural systems.[^iv71]

#### Pricing for Restructured Utilities

Similar to vertically integrated utilities, except power supply charges are separately stated. Monthly fixed charges should not exceed customer-specific costs. Most distribution costs should be reflected on a TOU/CPP or TOU/PTR basis. Consumers desiring a non-differentiated price may be able to contract with a competitive energy supplier to accept the risk of high costs during some periods and bundle the cost of risk management into a contracted price.[^iv72]

### Rate Design for Customers with Distributed Energy Resources (DER)

#### DER Compensation Framework

Historically, customers were not given any price signal about the value DER provides the electric system. This changed in the late 1970s with the passage of PURPA, which provided for mandatory purchase of power from customer-sited generation.[^v93] A number of compensation mechanisms have been considered: value-to-grid approaches using avoided costs, and distribution credits.[^v94] Locational value of DER provides incentives for DER in congested areas, which can defer or avoid costly upgrades.[^v95][^v96] Some of the earliest energy-efficiency programs were directed at locations with impending reliability problems due to distribution system constraints.[^v97] Locational marginal pricing (LMP) provides a mechanism for revealing the cost of supplying the next unit of load at a specific location.[^v98]

The pragmatic way to reflect locational values to residential and small commercial consumers is through targeted incentives for peak load management, not complex retail rate designs. Candidate zones are those approaching the maximum capacity of the affected part of the grid, with low to moderate growth rates over the medium to long term.[^v99]

#### Recovery Strategies for DG Grid Adaptation Costs

In Hawaii, where grid modifications are more imminently needed, the PUC Chairman reached an agreement with Hawaiian Electric to resume rapid approval of solar connections.[^v100] Hawaii's overall plan to adapt to high levels of DG is motivated in large measure by a determination to dramatically reduce fuel oil consumption.[^v101] Grid modifications associated with DG include:

- **Line transformer**: Must be sized for maximum flow in either direction. A customer-specific transformer charge is one approach.
- **Circuit capacity**: Upgrades only needed when installed solar significantly exceeds circuit capacity.
- **Smart inverters**: Hawaii requires new inverters to "ride through" system disturbances.
- **Voltage regulation**: High solar penetration may require additional voltage regulators.
- **Substations**: At very high penetration (20%+ customers with PV), power may flow "backward" through substations.
- **Generation**: Most regions can adapt through flexible generation, energy efficiency, DR, time-varying prices, and inter-regional cooperation.[^v102]
- **Demand response**: Grid-integrated water heating and storage air conditioning can add flexibility.[^v103]

Guidelines for equitable treatment of solar and other residential consumers:

- **Customer charges**: Should not exceed customer-specific costs.
- **Energy charges**: Should generally be time-varying in both directions.
- **Minimum bills**: Not a particularly desirable rate design as a rule.[^v105]
- **Demand or connected load charges**: Only relevant for recovery of line transformer costs (~$1/kW/month). Never appropriate for upstream distribution costs.[^v104]
- **Low-cost utilities** (average revenue <$0.10/kWh): May need inclining block rates.
- **Most (average-cost) utilities** ($0.10–$0.20/kWh): Conventional NEM is appropriate.
- **High-cost utilities** (>$0.20/kWh): May need to reflect distribution charges separately.

#### EVs with AMI

A utility with AMI has many options: simple TOU rate, multi-period TOU with super-off-peak, CPP, or RTP. Grid-operator controlled charging could allow the EV owner to request an "economy charge" by a defined time, and the grid operator would use the lowest-cost available hours.

#### Public Charging Stations and Time-Differentiated Pricing

Pricing schemes include free charging, hourly parking, and time-differentiated pricing per kWh. Regulators and legislators need to consider whether the public interest is served by imposing regulation on EV charging,[^v106] or whether that will discourage the availability of EV charging stations.

#### Vehicle-to-Grid and Full System Integration

One of the great promises of EVs is that they will become fully grid-integrated, providing a market for off-peak power, a source for on-peak power, and multiple ancillary services.[^v107] This requires a combination of sophisticated charging units in vehicles, complex pricing, and a very smart grid.[^v108] Experiments are underway in Maryland and Delaware through a partnership among Honda, the University of Delaware, and Delmarva Power.[^v109]

### Green Pricing

Green pricing is an optional utility rate or service allowing customers to support greater renewable energy investment,[^v110] typically by paying a premium on their electric bills. Funds are used to develop renewable energy projects or purchase renewable energy certificates (RECs).[^v111] Approximately 850 utilities offer a green pricing option.[^v112] Green power customers should be exempt from fuel adjustment mechanisms.[^v113]

### Customer-Provided Ancillary Services

Providing rates with time-varying energy, capacity, and ancillary service components could compensate DG, energy efficiency, and DR programs for newly recognized values.[^v114] A smart grid's built-in communications infrastructure could enable the system operator to manage water heaters and distributed resources to provide reactive power, voltage support, and other ancillary services.[^v115] For this to happen with PV systems, the deployment of smart inverters would be required.[^v116] EPRI is developing standards that set key functionalities for smart inverters to allow them to communicate with the grid.[^v117] Pragmatically, it makes little sense to offer rates to residential and small commercial consumers that include separate charges for ancillary services, but aggregators should be well-positioned to deal with detailed tariffs. Most programs reward customers via fixed monthly bill credits in exchange for allowing utility or DR aggregator control over certain loads. Flexible arrangements with customer override functions greatly improve satisfaction and participation rates, with very small impact on program performance.[^v118]

## VI. Other Issues in Rate Design

### Alternative Futures: Smart and Not-So-Smart

**The Smart Future**: Extensive use of technology to help consumers manage energy costs, with utility pricing that enables savings. Consumers will have smart homes with smart appliances. Utilities will use AMI for two-way communication. Required actions include: adopting time-varying and dynamic rate designs; implementing revenue regulation; new building energy codes; requiring smart inverters and batteries; and appliance standards requiring control technologies.

**Not-So-Smart Future**: SFV rates with high fixed charges. Adverse impacts include:

- **Energy efficiency**: Higher fixed charges lower the per-kWh rate, undermining incentives to invest in efficiency.
- **Competitive impact on renewables**: Lower per-kWh charges cut into potential savings from PV investments, potentially driving customers to go totally off-grid.
- **Low-income households**: Typical households below 150% of the federal poverty level use 3%–9% less electricity than average;[^vi119] most low-income customers' bills rise under SFV.
- **Apartment and urban dwellers**: Smaller units' bills rise while larger dwellings' bills go down, despite the fact that multi-family buildings are cheaper to serve.
- **Small-use residential consumers**: These customers are "less peaky" and would generally benefit from time-varying pricing.[^vi121]

### Addressing Revenue Erosion

Utility cost recovery and revenue stability can be addressed in four different ways:

1. **Cost of Capital** ("Let the Capital Markets Do It"): Without revenue regulation, utilities face a higher cost of capital, leading to higher costs to consumers.

2. **Incentive Regulation** ("Incentivize Management"): Performance-based ratemaking provides financial rewards for achieving goals such as energy efficiency and renewable generation deployment.[^vi122]

3. **Revenue Regulation and Decoupling** ("Passive Auto-Pilot"):[^vi123] Widely used to insulate utilities from revenue impacts due to sales variations.[^vi124] The best option because:
   - Rates remain volumetric, preserving incentives for efficiency
   - Customer bills remain predictable and linked to usage
   - Small-use customers are not disproportionately affected
   - Utilities, regulators, and intervenors avoid the cost of annual rate cases
   - If actual revenues exceed authorized, customers can see a rate decrease
   - Provides transparency
   - Periodic general rate case review ensures balance

4. **High fixed charges**: The most adverse impacts on consumers, the environment, the economy, and society.

### Bill Simplification

In many states, the utility bill has become a rather dense tangle of line items. Utilities should be required to display the "effective" rate to customers, including all surcharges, credits, and taxes, so consumers can measure the value of investing in energy efficiency.

### Customer Revenue Responsibilities

As customers utilize greater energy efficiency and deploy more PV, the reductions in their bills can allocate greater cost recovery responsibility to other customers. This is often described as a cross-subsidy; however, the system for allocating costs among customers has always been dynamic, reflecting changing characteristics over time. This is especially true given there is no single "correct" method of allocating costs.

### Changes in Customer Characteristics and Class Assignments

Even if all customers are equipped with smart meters, not all will be in the same position to deploy smart appliances or finance DG — especially renters. Regulators should focus on rate designs that maximize the value of smart technologies while assuring no customer group is left out.

DG customers, under time-varying rates, will pay the full cost of any service they receive from the utility. Non-DG customers will likely see some increase in distribution prices but overall costs may decline. Customers who go "off grid" do not deliver many of the benefits that grid-connected DG customers provide.

## VII. Conclusions

The future of the electric sector will likely include storage, microgrids, EVs, and more DER. Rate design will be an important driver of success in the transition to a clean power system. Utilities, customers, and third-party service providers will need the tools to manage the grid as efficiently as possible.

For DG customers specifically, bidirectional rates with TOU pricing may offer one of the best solutions. The DG customer pays the full retail rate for any power consumed and is compensated based on the same time periods, either using the retail rate or on a value basis.

Utility rate designs will have to more appropriately reflect the cost of electricity provided by the utility and the benefits provided to the utility system by customers. Smart rate designs will need to address not only the amount consumed but also when it is consumed and its impact on costs and other customers.

Viewed as a quick fix to lost revenues, SFV rates with high monthly fixed charges are increasingly being proposed. **SFV is not a step forward, but a step backward.** It does not align cost to cost causation and has adverse consequences for urban, multi-family, low-income, and low-use customers as well as those who invest in energy efficiency, demand response, and distributed generation. By de-linking customer use from the customer's bill, SFV encourages wasteful consumption and sends misleading, incomplete price signals.

The role of regulation in power sector transformation will be to develop pathways that lead to smarter solutions that optimize the value of interconnection and two-way communication for the customer and the grid. Other instruments include performance-based regulation (PBR) and integrated distribution grid planning (IDGP). The speed at which change takes place will vary from jurisdiction to jurisdiction.

## Glossary

**Adjusted Test Year**: A utility's investment, expense, and sales information used to allocate costs among customer classes, adjusted for known and measurable changes.

**Adjustment Clause**: A rate adjustment mechanism to recover changes in expenses or capital expenditures between rate cases (e.g., fuel and purchased power adjustment clause).

**Advanced Metering Infrastructure (AMI)**: A combination of smart meters, communication systems, SCADA, and MDMS enabling high temporal granularity metering, two-way communication, and potential end-use control.

**Aggregation**: Bundling of multiple customers or loads to achieve economies of scale in energy markets.

**Allocation**: The assignment of utility costs to customers, customer groups, or unbundled services based on cost causation principles.

**Allowed Rate of Return**: The weighted cost of capital used by the regulator to determine a utility's revenue requirement.

**Ancillary Service**: Services offered by system operators addressing reliability and operational requirements, including voltage control, reactive power, frequency control, spinning reserves, and standby power.

**Avoided Cost**: The cost of providing additional power, including the cost of the next power plant a utility would have to build, plus augmenting reserves, additional T&D, environmental costs, and line losses.

**Baseline Rate**: A rate allowing customers to buy a set allowance of energy at lower rates than additional usage.

**Baseload Generation**: Electricity generating units most economically run for extended hours, characterized by low fuel costs and high capital costs.

**Capacity**: The ability to generate, transport, process, or utilize power, measured in watts (kW or MW).

**Capacity Firming**: The use of low-cost options (DR, interruptibility, emergency generators) to supply capacity when other resources are unavailable.

**Circuit**: A wire conducting electricity from one point to another.

**Class Peak Demand**: The combined demand of all customers in a single rate class at its maximum point in time.

**Classification**: A step in an embedded cost of service study separating costs into demand-related, energy-related, joint, and customer-related categories.

**Cogeneration/Combined Heat and Power (CHP)**: A method of producing power in conjunction with providing process heat.

**Coincident Peak Demand**: The combined demand of customers relative to the peak demand of the system.

**Connected Load Charge**: A rate design where customers pay a fixed charge based on the capacity of their service interconnection.

**Conservation Voltage Reduction (CVR)**: Using smart distribution grid sensors to maintain distribution voltages at a uniform level just above the minimum required.

**Cost Allocation**: Division of a utility's cost of service among customer classes.

**Cost of Capital**: The costs a utility incurs borrowing money and issuing equity to shareholders.

**Cost of Service Study (COSS)**: A mathematical allocation of the utility's revenue requirement among customer classes.

**Critical Peak Pricing (CPP)**: A rate design with extraordinarily high prices during a limited number of declared critical peak demand periods.

**Curtailment**: A reduction in customer load in response to prices or system reliability threats.

**Customer Charge/Basic Charge/Service Charge**: A fixed charge each billing period, typically covering metering, meter reading, and billing costs.

**Customer Class**: A collection of customers sharing common usage or interconnection characteristics.

**Customer-Related Cost**: Costs that vary directly with the number of customers (metering, billing, customer service).

**Customer-Sited Generation**: Generation located at a customer's site; a form of distributed generation.

**Declining Block Rate**: Blocks of energy usage have declining prices as usage increases. Largely fallen out of favor.

**Decoupling**: Revenue regulation in which nonvariable costs are recovered through a prescribed revenue level, regardless of sales volume.

**Default Rate/Default Service/Standard Service Offer (SSO)**: The rate schedule paid if a different option is not affirmatively chosen.

**Demand**: The rate at which power is being consumed, measured in kW or MW.

**Demand Charge**: A charge based on metered demand, usually expressed in $/kW.

**Demand Ratchet**: A scheme charging demand based on the highest metered demand over multiple billing cycles.

**Demand-Related Cost**: Costs associated primarily with maximum system demand.

**Demand Response (DR)**: Reduction in energy use in response to reliability concerns or increased prices.

**Distributed Energy Resources (DER)**: Any resource at or near customer loads that generates energy or reduces consumption.

**Distributed Generation (DG)**: Any electricity generator located at or near customer loads.

**Distribution**: Delivery of electricity via low-voltage lines (usually 34 kV and below).

**Dynamic Pricing**: Changing prices for electricity reflecting actual wholesale market conditions.

**Economic Dispatch**: Utilization of existing generating resources to serve load as inexpensively as possible.

**Embedded Cost**: A cost already incurred or unavoidable in the future.

**Energy**: A unit of demand consumed over time, expressed in kWh or MWh.

**Energy Charge**: A price component based on energy consumed ($/kWh or $/MWh).

**Energy Conservation**: Behavioral changes or changes in usage patterns that reduce energy.

**Energy Efficiency**: Deployment of end-use appliances achieving equal or greater value while reducing energy required.

**Energy Time-Shift**: Purchasing inexpensive energy when prices are low to store and use or sell when prices are high.

**Energy-Related Cost**: Any cost categorized as energy in a cost of service study.

**Externalities**: Costs or benefits not reflected in booked utility costs (e.g., health-care costs from air pollution).

**Fixed Charge**: Any fee or charge that does not vary with consumption.

**Fixed Cost**: An accounting term for costs that do not vary within a certain period. From a regulatory and economics perspective, all utility costs are variable in the long run.

**Flat Rate**: A uniform price per kWh for all levels of consumption.

**Frequency**: Cycles per second of an AC electric system (60 Hz in North America).

**Fuel Cost**: The cost of fuel used to create electricity.

**Green Power**: An offering of environmentally preferred power at a premium.

**Grid Integration**: Management of variable power flows maintaining power quality, voltage, and frequency stability.

**IEEE 1547**: Industry standard governing engineering and performance criteria for interconnection of customer-sited generation.

**Incentive Regulation/Performance-Based Regulation (PBR)**: Regulation with specific performance targets, rewarded financially for meeting or exceeding them.

**Inclining Block Rate**: Blocks of energy usage have increasing prices as usage increases.

**Incremental Cost**: A cost study method based on the short-run cost of augmenting an existing system.

**Independent Power Plant (IPP)**: A power plant operating in a competitive market, not directly in regulated utility rates.

**Integrated Resource Planning (IRP)**: A public planning process evaluating demand and supply-side resources to develop least-total-cost options.

**Interval Meter**: A meter measuring and recording usage over a defined period of time.

**Line Transformer**: A transformer directly providing service to a customer.

**Load**: The combined demand for electricity placed on the system.

**Load Following**: Matching variations in load by adjusting generation supply or loads.

**Long-Run Marginal Costs**: The long-run costs of the next unit of electricity, including new power plant, additional T&D, reserves, losses, and environmental costs.

**Losses**: Energy and power lost in the operation of an electric system.

**Lost Revenue Adjustment Mechanism (LRAM)**: A mechanism allowing a utility to recover sales margins lost from energy efficiency or renewable energy programs.

**Marginal Cost**: Long-run costs of the next unit of electricity produced.

**Meter Data Management System (MDMS)**: A computer and control system gathering metering information from smart meters.

**Microgrid**: A localized grouping of electricity sources and loads that can disconnect and function autonomously.

**Minimum Bill**: A rate design charging a minimum amount in return for a designated amount of energy.

**Net Energy Metering (NEM)**: A rate design allowing a customer with DG to receive a bill credit at the full retail rate for energy injected into the system.

**Non-Coincident Demand (NCD)**: A customer's maximum energy demand during a billing period, even if different from system peak.

**Off-Peak**: The period when system costs are generally lower and reliability is not an issue.

**On-Peak**: The period when customer demand is higher than normal and system costs are higher.

**Opt-In**: Customers are placed on an alternative rate only if they actively choose it.

**Opt-Out**: Customers are automatically placed on the new rate unless they actively choose to stay on the existing one.

**Peak Demand**: The maximum demand by a customer or group during a specific period.

**Peak-Time Rebate (PTR)**: A rate design providing a bill credit for reducing usage below a baseline during peak demand.

**Photovoltaic (PV) Systems**: Electric generating systems using photovoltaic cells to generate electricity from sunlight.

**Power Factor**: The fraction of power actually used compared with total apparent power supplied.

**Power Quality**: Technical metrics applied to voltage stability, frequency, waveform, and other details.

**Real-Time Pricing (RTP)**: Rates adjusting as frequently as hourly based on wholesale costs or actual generation costs.

**Reliability**: The ability of the electric system to provide continuous service over time.

**Renewable Energy Certificate (REC)**: Documentation of energy produced by a renewable resource, tradable separately from the energy.

**Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS)**: A requirement that utilities meet a specified percentage of power supply using qualified renewable resources.

**Revenue Regulation**: An approach allowing a utility to collect a target revenue level regardless of sales volume.

**Revenue Requirement**: The annual revenues a utility is entitled to collect (O&M + depreciation + taxes + return on rate base).

**Seasonal Rate**: A rate higher during peak-usage months of the year.

**Service Drop**: A transformer, conductor, pole, or underground facilities connecting a single customer to the electric system.

**Smart Appliance**: An appliance capable of communicating with a data acquisition and control system.

**Smart Grid**: An integrated network of sophisticated meters, computer controls, information exchange, automation, and pricing options.

**Smart Meter**: A meter with electronics enabling recording in short time intervals and two-way communication.

**Smart Technology**: The collection of smart meters, smart appliances, SCADA, and MDMS.

**Spinning Reserve**: Any energy resource callable within a designated period of time for balancing loads and resources.

**Straight-Fixed/Variable Rate (SFV)**: A rate design recovering all short-run fixed costs in a fixed charge and only short-run variable costs in a per-unit charge.

**Substation**: A facility with a transformer stepping voltage down, to which circuits or customers may be connected.

**Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition (SCADA)**: Sensors, meters, communications equipment, and computers monitoring and controlling the electric system.

**System Peak Demand**: The maximum demand placed on the electric system at a single point in time.

**Tariff**: A listing of rates, charges, and other terms of service for a customer class.

**Time-of-Use Rate (TOU)**: Rates varying by time of day and day of the week.

**Transformer**: A device that raises or lowers voltage in an electric system.

**Used and Useful**: A regulatory concept for determining whether utility plant is eligible for inclusion in rate base.

**Value of Solar Tariff (VOST)**: A tariff paying for solar power injection based on its value (avoided energy, capacity, O&M, losses, reserves, social costs, less increased costs).

**Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G)**: Treating EVs as a distributed resource, allowing system operators to withdraw or store power in them.

**Weighted Cost of Capital**: A composite cost rate reflecting debt and equity costs in proportion to the capital structure.
